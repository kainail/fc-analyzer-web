# CLOSER + NEPQ Framework

This file documents the underlying frameworks the methodology is built on, in the form they have been adapted for the gym's PT consultations. Read this when scoring asks, needs, yesterdays, or close mechanics — it gives you the reference language to evaluate against.

## CLOSER (modified for PT consultations)

CLOSER is a structured sales framework. The gym uses a modified version that emphasizes qualifying depth over closing technique. The acronym, in this version:

- **C**larify why they're here
- **L**abel them with a problem
- **O**verview their past pains (yesterdays)
- **S**ell the vision
- **E**xplain away concerns (objections, ideally pre-handled)
- **R**einforce the decision

The first three steps — Clarify, Label, Overview — are the qualifying core. They map to the Asks, Needs, and Yesterdays stages of the consultation flow. If these three are executed deeply, the rest of the framework is mostly mechanical.

### Clarify

Ask why they are interested. The questions are direct:
- "What made you come in today?"
- "What made you reach out?"
- "What's your goal right now?"
- "Why is your goal important to you?"

The goal is to surface both the *what* (the goal) and the *why* (the emotional driver behind the goal). A rep who collects the what without digging for the why has stopped at clarify and not earned the right to label.

### Label

After clarifying, the rep labels the prospect with a problem:

```
"So this is your goal, does that sound about right?"
```

The label is in the rep's words. It frames the situation as solvable. The "does that sound about right" forces explicit confirmation.

This is then followed by the transition to yesterdays:

```
"What are you currently doing to achieve those goals,
and how is that working for you?"
```

The honest version of this question reveals that whatever they are currently doing is not working — which is why they are here.

### Overview (yesterdays)

The rep explores the prospect's past pains:
- "What have you tried so far?"
- "How long did you do it for?"
- "How long ago?"
- "How did that work for you?"
- "What else have you tried?"

The reframe at the end: it was not their fault. They were missing a piece or two of the equation. The rep is the missing piece.

This is the pain cycle. Done well, it produces material the rep will reference repeatedly throughout the rest of the call.

### Sell

After qualifying, the rep transitions to selling — which in the gym's version means delivering the 12-month diagram and pre-framing guarantees. The key principle: build excitement, paint future images, use social proof.

For the diagram specifically, the rep is imagining the prospect's likely concerns and explaining them away preemptively rather than waiting for them to surface as objections.

### Explain (objections, ideally pre-handled)

The framework recognizes that objections may still appear. Common categories:

- **Price** ("too expensive"): always the rep's fault if it surfaces. The Ferrari analogy: "If I tried to sell you a Ferrari for $5,000, you'd be suspicious — because the price doesn't match the value. The same is true in reverse." The fix is upstream: better value communication.
- **Decision-maker** ("I need to ask my spouse"): handle through "past agreements" framing. "Does your spouse know you're struggling with your health right now? Do they approve of that? Then why would they be against you doing this for yourself?"
- **Stalls / procrastination** ("I need to think about it"): qualify the stall directly. "Can the product meet your needs? If you worked with us, would you have a greater chance of getting where you want to be?" If yes, the only remaining question is budget — and the rep can offer to delay payment.

The pattern across all objection handling: get curious before you reframe. Understand what the objection actually means before answering it. Most objections are smokescreens for a different concern the prospect has not surfaced.

### Reinforce

After the close, reinforce the decision. Handwritten postcards. Personalized welcome videos. The rep's own coach reaching out with confirmed appointment times. The point is to make the sale stick.

## NEPQ frames (Jeremy Miner influence)

NEPQ — Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questioning — is Jeremy Miner's framework. The gym's methodology incorporates several specific NEPQ moves:

### Status, outcome, and consequence frames

NEPQ distinguishes between three types of qualifying questions:

- **Status questions**: "What are you currently doing?" — establish the present
- **Outcome questions**: "Where do you want to be?" — establish the desired future
- **Consequence questions**: "What happens if nothing changes?" — establish the cost of staying put

A rep who only asks status and outcome questions has a 2-D picture. The consequence question — what happens if you don't do anything — is the lever that creates urgency without manufacturing it. It surfaces the prospect's own awareness of the cost of inaction.

### The "hidden dialogue" (tone)

How you say something is what you say. NEPQ emphasizes vocal control:
- Voice rises for questions
- Voice lowers for important statements
- Pauses are used deliberately, not filled

The principle: if the rep believes what they are selling, the tone adjusts naturally. Weak tone usually signals weak conviction underneath.

### Curiosity-first objection handling

Every NEPQ objection sequence opens with curiosity, not a counter:

- "I need to think about it" → "Can I ask, what specifically are you wanting to think through?"
- "Too expensive" → "Is it the cost itself, or more like you're not sure if it's worth it for what you'd be getting?"
- "I need to ask my spouse" → "Are they into the idea too, or more like you just want to run it by them?"

The rep does not answer the objection. They qualify the objection itself to find what's underneath. This is consistent with the broader thesis that objections are often smokescreens — the surface phrasing is rarely the real concern.

### The "cap" on objection attempts

The methodology caps objection handling at two attempts. After two attempts, if the objection is still holding, the rep lets go cleanly:

```
"Totally fair — if you want to revisit just reach back out. We'd love to have you."
```

A third attempt is counterproductive. It damages trust and signals desperation. A rep who pushes past two attempts has prioritized this sale over future relationship — almost always a mistake.

## Hormozi influence

Hormozi's contribution to the methodology is primarily in offer construction and value framing:

- **Future-pacing language**: "imagine your success in a few months." The prospect mentally inhabits the outcome before paying for it.
- **Value > price**: the price is set, then the value is stacked until the value clearly exceeds the price. The pre-frame guarantees stage in the consultation flow does this work.
- **Social proof at the right moments**: not as a brag, but as evidence at the moment a claim is being made. "Most members training 3–4x per week start seeing impressive results around six months in" lands at the moment the rep is anchoring 4x.

## Putting it together

The gym's methodology is not three frameworks bolted together. It is a single coherent approach where:

- CLOSER provides the structural sequence
- NEPQ provides the questioning style and objection philosophy
- Hormozi provides the offer construction and value framing language

A rep who has internalized the methodology does not consciously think "now I'm doing CLOSER step 2." They simply ask the next right question, label when they have the material to label, explore yesterdays when the labeling is confirmed, and so on. The frameworks are scaffolding; mastery is fluency.

When scoring, do not check whether the rep used the literal vocabulary of any framework. Check whether they executed the *underlying moves* — surface the why, label and confirm, build identity contrast, communicate value through stories and metaphors, ask for the sale and stay silent. Those are the tests.
