# Sales Philosophy

This file is the lens. The rubric and scoring criteria do not make sense without it. Read this before scoring any consultation.

## The qualifying-as-leverage thesis

The first 15 minutes of a personal training consultation determine the outcome of the entire call. This is not a stylistic preference. It is a structural claim about how high-ticket in-person sales work.

The thesis runs like this. A prospect walks into a consultation with a surface goal ("I want to lose weight"), some collection of unspoken emotional drivers underneath that goal ("my daughter said something that hurt"), and a history of past attempts that failed for reasons they have not fully articulated to themselves. If the rep takes the surface goal at face value and moves toward the workout and the price, they are selling against an emotional baseline that is approximately neutral. The prospect feels mild interest. The price will then feel expensive relative to that mild interest.

If instead the rep digs past the surface goal until they reach the emotional why, then explores the prospect's past failures until the prospect articulates why they failed (almost always: lack of accountability), then labels the problem and gets explicit agreement, then paints a future image grounded in the prospect's specific stated identity — by the time the price comes up, the prospect is no longer evaluating a gym membership against neutral. They are evaluating a path away from the version of themselves that has been failing for years. That is a fundamentally different decision.

This is what "qualifying is leverage" means. Qualifying is not information gathering. It is the construction of the emotional and psychological context in which the price reveal will be received.

## The zombie analogy

Objections are downstream symptoms of qualifying gaps. The metaphor: objections are zombies. If you qualify well, you kill the zombies before they form. If you qualify poorly, you spend the back half of the call fighting zombies with a stick — handling objection after objection that should never have existed.

This has a sharp implication for how to score a call. **The presence of objections in the call is itself a diagnostic signal.** A call that closed at 4x with no objections is fundamentally stronger than a call that closed at 4x after the rep had to overcome three objections, even if the second rep "handled them well." The first rep qualified so deeply that there was nothing left to object to. The second rep recovered from a qualifying failure.

When you analyze a call and see an objection, your first job is not to score the handling. It is to identify the upstream moment in qualifying where the objection should have been pre-handled. Almost always, you will find a missed dig, a skipped label, a yesterdays exploration that stayed shallow, or a future image that ran generic.

This does not mean objection handling is unscored. It means the diagnostic flag for the objection lives at the upstream failure. The handling itself is secondary.

## Label and confirm

A pattern that recurs throughout the methodology, in every script, in every framework: surface the prospect's words, label them back as a problem the rep can solve, then get explicit agreement before moving on.

```
"So what I'm hearing is you're here because your energy's been low,
and you want to be more present with your kids — is that right?"
```

This sequence — paraphrase, label, ask for confirmation — is doing several things at once. It demonstrates listening. It allows the prospect to correct misunderstandings before they compound. It commits the prospect verbally to the framing the rep is about to build on. Without that commitment, the rep is building the rest of the call on assumptions the prospect never agreed to.

The "is that right?" is non-negotiable. Without explicit confirmation, the rep does not have a foundation to build on. Skipping it — even if the labeling itself was accurate — creates a fragile call where the prospect can later object on the grounds that "you didn't really understand what I was looking for."

When scoring, a rep who labels but does not pause for confirmation, or who labels and barrels past the prospect's response, has executed an incomplete pattern. This is a high-frequency failure mode worth specifically watching for.

## Yesterdays as identity contrast construction

The "yesterdays" stage of a consultation — the exploration of past attempts and past failures — is often misunderstood as empathy theater. It is not. It is the most important stage of the entire call after the emotional why is surfaced.

What yesterdays does mechanically: it builds the contrast between *who the prospect is now* (someone who has tried and failed, who has watched themselves not follow through, who is frustrated with a recurring pattern) and *who the prospect wants to become* (the version in the future image the rep will paint with the diagram). Without that contrast, the future image lands flat. With it, the future image becomes the only acceptable path away from a present they have just articulated as unacceptable.

Good yesterdays exploration extracts:
- Specific past attempts (not "I've tried stuff" but "I did keto for three months in 2022")
- Duration and recency (so the failures are concrete, not abstract)
- Why each attempt failed, in the prospect's own words
- A pattern the prospect themselves articulates ("I always start strong then fall off")

The reframe at the end of yesterdays — "it wasn't your fault, you were just missing a piece" — positions the rep as the missing piece without saying so directly. This is a Hormozi-style move: the prospect is not the problem; the absence of accountability is the problem; and accountability is what the rep is selling.

A rep who skips or shortens yesterdays loses the entire identity-contrast move. The diagram will land flat. The price will feel high. The close will require pushing.

## Callbacks: the test of whether discovery was real

A rep can ask all the right qualifying questions and still execute a poor call. The test of whether qualifying was real or performative is whether the material gets *used* later in the call.

Specifically, watch for callbacks at:
- The needs labeling moment ("so the missing piece all those other times was X — that's what we focus on")
- The diagram results phase ("unlike last time when you tried Y and Z happened, here we'll…")
- The pre-frame guarantees ("remember when you said you fell off because you didn't know what to do? That's why the custom workout matters")
- Any objection handling ("you mentioned earlier that consistency was the issue — that's exactly what this addresses")

A rep who collects yesterdays but never references them again has done discovery as a checklist rather than as ammunition. This is a specific, scoreable failure mode. Look for it.

## Mirroring (archetype as adaptation, not classification)

The rep should be reading the prospect throughout the call and adjusting accordingly. Pace, energy, language, depth, humor — all of these adapt to the person across the table.

The four archetypes (Social, Analytical, Supportive, Independent) are a teaching scaffold for new reps to recognize patterns. They are not boxes to put prospects into. A skilled rep does not consciously think "this is an Analytical, time to deploy the Analytical playbook." They simply adjust to the person and the conversation in real time.

When scoring, the question is not "did the rep correctly classify the archetype." The question is "did the rep read this person and adapt, or did they run their default style regardless of who was in front of them?" A rep delivering high-energy storytelling to a guarded, analytical prospect is mirroring poorly. A rep slowing down and validating with a hesitant prospect is mirroring well.

## Value communication: storytelling, metaphor, third-party framing, timeframe

The diagram is one place this shows up, but the underlying skills appear throughout the call. Specifically:

- **Storytelling**: rendering future success as a concrete scene the prospect can mentally inhabit ("imagine waking up and flexing in the mirror, your wife laughing"). Generic transformation language ("you'll feel better") does not do this work.
- **Metaphor**: making abstract concepts tangible. The body as a house. Accountability as Netflix. The Disneyland exhaustion. Each metaphor takes a fitness concept the prospect doesn't care about and reframes it through something they already understand.
- **Third-party framing**: invoking authority through "from the hundreds of clients I've worked with" or "most members who train 3–4x per week" — using social proof so the rep is not making the claim themselves. This is structurally different from self-promotion and lands harder.
- **Timeframe**: anchoring the prospect in a 12-month arc rather than a today's-price frame. The prospect is not buying a workout this week; they are buying a year of becoming someone different.

A rep who sells with feature lists and benefit statements but no stories, no metaphors, no third-party authority, and no timeframe is communicating value flatly. The prospect may agree intellectually but not emotionally. That gap is where price objections come from.

## Conviction: how you say it is what you say

Tone, pacing, and confidence carry roughly half the weight of the words themselves. A rep who knows the right scripts but delivers them apologetically, or rushes through silences, or fills awkward pauses, has undermined the words.

Specific markers of strong conviction:
- Voice lowers on important statements (price, guarantees, the close)
- Voice rises on questions that should land as questions (asks, follow-ups)
- The rep stays silent after asking for the sale, even if it gets uncomfortable
- The rep does not over-explain or apologize for price
- The rep believes what they are selling, audibly

Conviction is hard to score from a transcript alone — much of it is auditory. But strong patterns show up in word choice and structure: hedging language ("um, I think we have, like, a few options"), apologetic framing ("I know this might sound like a lot"), and pre-emptive justifications ("the reason it's this price is because…") all signal weak conviction. Their absence — clean, direct statements — signals strength.

## Momentum

Once the prospect agrees to move forward, the rep should not return to discovery. They should not introduce new variables. They should not over-explain. The single most common close-killing mistake is the rep, after getting a yes, continuing to talk and reopening considerations the prospect had already moved past.

When scoring close mechanics, watch specifically for: did the rep, after the prospect indicated agreement, immediately move to logistics (signing, payment method, scheduling) — or did they undercut their own close by adding more pitch?

## One question at a time

A discipline that recurs across every script in the gym's methodology. Stacking questions ("so what's your goal, and what have you tried, and how serious are you?") signals nervousness and produces shallow answers. Asking one question, pausing, listening, and following up produces depth.

When scoring qualifying, count question stacks. They are a high-frequency failure mode for newer reps and a reliable signal of insufficient discipline.

## Why this matters for scoring

Every principle above shows up in the rubric as a specific scoreable element. The rubric is the operationalization of this philosophy. If a rubric criterion ever feels arbitrary or unclear, return to this file — the principle behind the criterion is here.

The goal of the analyzer is not to enforce a checklist. It is to evaluate whether a rep is selling the way this methodology says high-ticket in-person sales should be sold, and to surface the highest-leverage thing for them to work on next. Everything else is in service of that.
