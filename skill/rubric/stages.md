# Stage Rubric

Each stage of a PT consultation gets a 1–10 score based on the criteria below. Every score must cite specific transcript evidence. Bare numbers are not acceptable.

## How to use this rubric

For each stage:

1. Read the relevant transcript section.
2. Compare against the criteria for the stage.
3. Identify what the rep did well and what they missed.
4. Anchor your score against the descriptors below (1–3, 4–6, 7–8, 9–10).
5. Cite specific transcript moments — exact quotes where possible, with timestamps if available.

If a stage was skipped entirely, score it 1 and note the skip explicitly. Do not score a skipped stage based on what was attempted later in the call.

## Universal scoring anchors

These descriptors apply to every stage. Use them to keep scores consistent across stages and across calls.

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **1–2** | Stage skipped, or attempted in a way that actively damaged the call |
| **3** | Stage attempted but executed shallowly; key elements missing |
| **4–5** | Stage executed but with significant gaps; foundation weak for what follows |
| **6** | Competent execution; minor gaps but workable |
| **7–8** | Strong execution; most elements present and well-handled |
| **9** | Excellent; rare execution that creates leverage for the rest of the call |
| **10** | Exceptional; reserve for executions that would be used as a teaching example |

A 7 should be common for skilled reps. A 9 should be rare. A 10 should be exceptional. If you find yourself scoring most stages 8 or above, you are likely calibrating too generously.

## Stage 1: Pre-frame and opening

**What you are scoring:** the first 1–3 minutes of the call. Did the rep open with discipline, build minimal rapport without burning time, and move directly into qualifying?

### What good looks like
- Brief casual rapport ("how's your day been") — under 60 seconds
- Direct transition into qualifying ("so what made you decide to get a gym membership?" or "why now?")
- No product pitching, no facility history, no premature feature lists
- Collaborative seating and posture (not adversarial)
- If a fitness questionnaire was used, it has been completed before the substantive conversation begins

### Failure modes
- Rep launches into facility history, equipment, or pricing before qualifying
- Rapport extended beyond what is comfortable, signaling avoidance of the substantive call
- Rep reads questionnaire questions verbatim instead of weaving them into conversation
- Stage compressed so heavily that it feels transactional rather than warm

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires a clean, direct transition into qualifying within the first few minutes. A 5 is competent but bloated rapport. Below 5 if the rep opened with product or pricing.

## Stage 2: Asks (surfacing the why)

**What you are scoring:** how deeply the rep dug past the surface goal to reach the emotional why.

### What good looks like
- Rep asks "why now?" or an equivalent question
- Rep follows up at least 2–3 times after the surface goal, each follow-up going deeper
- Rep asks consequence questions ("what happens if nothing changes?") in addition to outcome questions
- Rep reaches an emotional why — something tied to identity, relationships, fear, or specific events — not just a goal
- Rep asks one question at a time; does not stack questions

### Failure modes
- Rep accepts the surface goal ("I want to lose weight") without follow-up
- Rep stacks questions ("so what's your goal, what have you tried, and how serious are you?")
- Rep asks the right questions but does not pause for substantive answers; rushes through
- Rep reaches an emotional why but immediately moves on without acknowledging it
- Rep asks generic questions from a script without responsiveness to what the prospect said

### Specific things to look for
- Did "why now" appear, in some form?
- Did the rep ask about the cost of inaction (consequence frame)?
- Did the rep reach an answer that has emotional content (kids, partner, fear, specific event)?
- How many follow-up questions did the rep ask after the surface goal?

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires reaching the emotional why with multiple follow-ups. A 5 is asking the right questions but stopping shallow. Below 5 if the rep moved past the goal without digging.

This stage is weighted heavily in the overall analysis. A weak asks stage corrupts everything downstream.

## Stage 3: Needs (label and confirm)

**What you are scoring:** did the rep label the prospect's situation as a solvable problem and get explicit confirmation?

### What good looks like
- Rep paraphrases what the prospect said using the rep's own framing (not the prospect's exact words)
- The label frames the situation as a *problem* with a solution, not just a wish
- Rep asks "is that right?" or equivalent — explicit confirmation
- Rep waits for the prospect's response and adjusts the label if corrected
- Once confirmed, rep transitions to yesterdays cleanly

### Failure modes
- Rep paraphrases but does not ask for confirmation
- Rep labels using the prospect's exact words (just repeating, not framing)
- Rep gets a partial confirmation and moves on without re-labeling
- Rep skips labeling entirely and moves directly from asks to yesterdays
- Rep talks over the prospect's correction

### Specific things to look for
- Was there an explicit "is that right?" or equivalent?
- Did the prospect verbally confirm?
- If the prospect corrected, did the rep adjust and re-confirm?

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires a clean label-and-confirm with verbal agreement. A 5 is labeling without confirmation. Below 5 if labeling was skipped or so vague the prospect could not have confirmed anything specific.

## Stage 4: Yesterdays (identity contrast)

**What you are scoring:** how thoroughly the rep explored past attempts, how concrete the material became, and how it was reframed.

### What good looks like
- Rep extracts specific past attempts (not "I've tried stuff" but actual programs/timeframes)
- Rep gets duration and recency for past attempts
- Rep asks why each attempt failed and lets the prospect articulate it
- Rep allows the prospect to surface a pattern themselves ("I always start strong then fall off")
- Rep reframes the failures as not the prospect's fault — they were missing a piece
- Rep does not project an explanation onto the prospect

### Failure modes
- Rep asks "have you tried this before?" and accepts a vague yes/no
- Rep skips yesterdays and goes directly from needs to the diagram
- Rep tells the prospect why their past attempts failed instead of asking
- Rep collects yesterdays but executes the reframe weakly or not at all
- Yesterdays explored only for one past attempt when there were multiple

### Specific things to look for
- How many past attempts were surfaced?
- Did the prospect articulate why they failed, in their own words?
- Did the rep reframe with the "missing piece" move?
- Did the prospect articulate a pattern (recurring failure)?

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires concrete past attempts with reasons for failure, plus the missing-piece reframe. A 5 is surface-level past attempts without depth. Below 5 if yesterdays was skipped or compressed to under a minute.

This stage is weighted heavily. Yesterdays builds the identity contrast that makes the diagram and price land. Without it, the call has no foundation for the value communication that follows.

## Stage 5: Workout

**What you are scoring:** did the rep build rapport on the floor, personalize the workout, and end with a clean pre-frame for the close?

### What good looks like
- Workout is tied to the prospect's stated weak points or goals
- Rep keeps the workout fun; humor and conversation flow
- Rep ties exercises back to the prospect's situation ("this movement is what'll let you return to that hobby pain-free")
- A moment where the prospect surprises themselves (improving an injury, executing something they thought they couldn't)
- Workout ends with a clean pre-frame: "this is our last set, then we'll head back to the office, review, and I'd love to show you what we have"
- Rep offers water or towel at the end

### Failure modes
- Generic workout not tied to the prospect's situation
- Rep tries to "kill" the prospect with intensity instead of focusing on personalization
- Workout dominated by silence; no rapport built
- No pre-frame for the close; transition is abrupt

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires a personalized workout with rapport and a clean pre-frame. A 5 is workout executed without personalization. The workout stage is hard to score from transcript alone (much of the rapport is non-verbal) — calibrate based on what verbal exchange shows.

## Stage 6: Pre-frame guarantees

**What you are scoring:** did the rep load value before showing price?

### What good looks like
- Transition questions ("how'd that workout feel?", "can you see how this leads to your goals?")
- Rep walks through guarantees with conviction: customized programs, meal plans, biometric scans, money-back guarantee
- Each guarantee tied back, where possible, to something the prospect surfaced earlier (callbacks)
- Money-back guarantee discussed in detail, not glossed over
- Pre-frame happens *before* any price is shown

### Failure modes
- Rep skips guarantees and goes directly to price
- Guarantees listed flatly without conviction or detail
- Guarantees not tied to prospect's stated situation (no callbacks)
- Money-back guarantee mentioned in passing instead of emphasized
- Rep shows price first, then tries to justify it with guarantees retroactively

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires guarantees walked through with conviction and tied to discovery. A 5 is guarantees mentioned but flat. Below 5 if guarantees were skipped or price was shown first.

## Stage 7: Price anchor

**What you are scoring:** did the rep give a professional recommendation, anchor at 4x (or appropriate higher frequency), and tie the recommendation to the prospect's stated situation?

### What good looks like
- Rep explicitly recommends a frequency ("based on everything you shared, I'd recommend 4x/week")
- Recommendation is grounded in something the prospect said (callback)
- 4x is the anchor unless genuinely inappropriate for the prospect
- All options are shown after the anchor, not before
- Rep explains why this frequency, not just what it costs

### Failure modes
- Rep shows all options without an explicit recommendation
- Rep anchors at 1x or 2x by default
- Rep recommends a frequency but does not tie it to the prospect's why
- Rep apologizes for or hedges the recommendation
- Rep treats the price reveal as adversarial rather than collaborative

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires an explicit anchored recommendation tied to the prospect's situation. A 5 is recommendation without anchoring or callback. Below 5 if no recommendation was made.

---

## Stage 8: Close

**What you are scoring:** did the rep ask for the sale cleanly and stay silent?

### What good looks like
- Rep makes a clear, direct ask ("ready to get started?")
- Rep stays silent after asking; does not fill the pause
- Silence lasts at least 5 seconds (often shows up in transcript as a gap)
- Rep does not re-pitch, re-justify, or add new variables after the ask
- If the prospect goes for the lowest package, rep reframes and pushes for higher frequency rather than settling

### Failure modes
- Rep asks for the sale and immediately fills the silence with more pitch
- Rep asks indirectly or hedges ("would you maybe want to think about getting started?")
- Rep accepts the lowest package without trying to anchor up
- Rep introduces new variables after the prospect indicated agreement (momentum killed)
- Rep apologizes for the price during the close

### Specific things to look for
- Was there a clear, unhedged ask?
- Did the rep stay silent?
- If the prospect went low, did the rep reframe?

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires a clean ask with held silence. A 5 is a clear ask but silence broken too early. Below 5 if the ask was hedged or absent.

---

## Stage 9: Reinforce

**What you are scoring:** did the rep reinforce the sale, sign the prospect up immediately, collect EFT cleanly, and walk through contract policies without undermining the close?

### What good looks like
- Rep shows excitement after the sale
- Rep signs the prospect up immediately, does not chat (which can lose the sale)
- EFT collected with the savings/checking framing, not card/account
- Contract policies walked through with reframes ("we have a 50% cancellation fee, but you can pause for three months per year — we do this as a commitment to you")
- Ends with energy: high-five, copy of workout, follow-up plan
- If no-close: rep still ends warmly, sends thank-you message after

### Failure modes
- Rep gets the yes and then keeps chatting, reopening considerations
- Rep collects payment fumbling through method
- Rep walks through cancellation/contract terms without reframes (negative-only)
- No follow-up message planned
- Energy drops after the sale, signaling the rep was performing during the close rather than genuinely excited

### Scoring guidance
A 7+ requires immediate signup with energy and clean payment collection. A 5 is signup completed but with friction. Below 5 if reinforce dropped energy or undermined the sale.

## Output requirement

For each stage, the analyzer's JSON output must include:

```json
{
  "stage": "asks",
  "score": 6,
  "evidence_quotes": [
    "Direct quote from transcript with rough timestamp",
    "Another supporting moment"
  ],
  "what_worked": "Specific element the rep executed well",
  "what_was_missed": "Specific element the rep failed to execute",
  "upstream_consequences": "How this stage's quality shaped what happened later (optional, for stages 2-4)"
}
```

The `upstream_consequences` field is especially important for asks, needs, and yesterdays — these are the foundational stages, and tracing how their quality affected later stages is the analyzer's most valuable diagnostic move.
